How-to guide: Remedial Math Program

Note: These resources are currently available in the Romanian language.

Who this guide is for

Teachers, school leaders, and local education teams (district/county) who want to set up a structured, small-group remedial math program for lower-secondary students (grades 5–8) with weak foundations. It also helps NGOs or ministry teams coordinating training, diagnostics, and monitoring.

Before you start

  • Secure the core package and templates. Make sure you have the student program units, teacher lesson skeleton, and the tutorial structure materials. These include a consistent lesson pattern (theory + practice + quick checks) and references to diagnostics and teacher practice skills.
  • Identify your team. Assign at least one teacher/facilitator per small group (15–20 students) and name a school lead to coordinate diagnostics, scheduling, and data use.
  • Plan the diagnostic. Schedule a BRIO baseline for target students (grades 5–8 domains) and set a date for regrouping based on results. The guide and tutorial outline point to using BRIO both to place students and to track progress over time.
  • Get teachers ready. Share the Foundational Teaching Skills / AFP overview. The teacher module covers 11 core skills (e.g., checking for understanding, giving clear directions, feedback, adapting instruction) across Classroom Climate and Instruction—with theory, application, and a short check at the end of each lesson.

Step-by-step plan

Step 1 — Identify students and run the BRIO diagnostic

What to do:

  • Select the target cohort (e.g., entering lower-secondary students with weak math skills, or students flagged by teachers).
  • Administer the BRIO diagnostic that covers grades 5–8 foundational content.
  • Record subgroup needs (e.g., fractions/operations, early algebra, geometry) and note any systematic gaps (e.g., “real numbers” strand and links to data/graphs—pointed out in the BRIO analysis).   

Why it matters: You will teach at the right level from day one and avoid pacing that’s either too slow or too fast. It also prevents focusing only on basics: students can work on priority gaps while moving into problem solving—an approach supported in the pedagogy notes. 

Step 2 — Form small instructional groups and set the schedule

What to do:

  • Create groups of ~15–20 students with similar needs.
  • Set a 30-week plan (approx. 1.5–2 hours/week), mapping your school calendar to the 10 learning units (from numbers and operations to algebra/geometry to data & probability). Use your local constraints to combine or split sessions. (The program material enumerates the units and time budgets.) 

Why it matters: Consistency beats intensity for catch-up. Short, regular sessions help students rebuild fluency and confidence while keeping the core school timetable intact.

Step 3 — Prepare teachers on AFP/Foundational Skills (Teach-aligned)

What to do:

  • Run a short prep using the AFP module: it introduces 11 teachable skills and how to bring them into remedial lessons (e.g., Checking for Understanding, Demonstrate & Practice—“I do, we do, you do”, Clear Directions, Feedback, Adapting Instruction). Lessons mix theory + application and include quick checks; teachers pass the module with a minimum score (70%) to ensure common practice.     

Example moves for remedial lessons:

  • Checking for Understanding: 2-item exit ticket at lesson end; thumbs-up/sideways checks mid-task.
  • Demonstrate & Practice: Teacher models one worked example → students complete two similar examples with prompts → independent item.
  • Feedback: “Say what worked + what to fix + how,” in one sentence per student; rotate who receives feedback each mini-cycle.
  • Adaptation: Provide a visual scaffold (diagram, manipulatives) for students who stall on symbolic problems.

Step 4 — Use the standard lesson skeleton for every unit

What to do:

  • Adopt the lesson skeleton included in your materials: it prompts you to pull diagnostic results (e.g., BRIO) into planning, present minimal “theory at a glance,” run guided practice, and end with a quick check. There’s a sample “Circle” theory block you can mirror for other topics.   

Why it matters: Students see a predictable routine and teachers avoid over-teaching theory. The template ensures time for guided practice and checks for understanding.

Concrete example (Geometry—Circles):

  • Warm-up (5 min): Identify radius and diameter on a drawn circle.
  • Direct instruction (10 min): Model drawing circles with a compass; label r and d.
  • Guided practice (15 min): Students draw three circles of different radii; measure diameters; compare r↔d.
  • Independent (10 min): Students solve 3 items (e.g., “Given d=6 cm, state r”).
  • Exit ticket (5 min): 2 questions (identify radius on a diagram; draw a circle of given radius).
  • (This follows the provided lesson skeleton with theory + practice + quick check.) 

Step 5 — Address known weak areas from Teach and BRIO evidence

What to do:

  • From Teach observations: improve feedback quality, deeper thinking prompts, student autonomy/persistence, and social-collaboration skills during tasks. Plan one small improvement focus each week (e.g., write 2 higher-order prompts per lesson; schedule peer-explain moments). 
  • From BRIO: watch the real numbers strand and its links to data/graphs—these areas show heavy struggle; interleave quick graphing or data-read tasks while teaching numbers/algebra. 

Why it matters: You’ll target what evidence shows students and teachers most need, not just “cover” the unit list.

Step 6 — Track learning with simple checks and mid-course re-grouping

What to do:

  • End every lesson with a 2-item check tied to that day’s objective.
  • At Unit 3/6/9, run a short progress check (5–10 items)—then regroup if a subset needs more practice on a core idea.
  • Close the program with a post-diagnostic to show growth and identify students needing continuing support.

Why it matters: Frequent low-stakes checks improve retention and let you adjust instruction quickly—core to the AFP module’s “checking for understanding” and “adapting instruction.” 

Timeline examples

  • Quick rollout (pilot): 2–3 weeks to set up: Week 1 diagnostics + grouping; Week 2 teacher AFP prep; Week 3 first lessons.
  • Full program: 30 weeks of instruction, with milestone checks at roughly every 10 weeks; align with the 10 program units and your school breaks.

Roles and responsibilities

  • Teachers/Trainers — Teach the small groups; apply the AFP moves (clear directions, “I do–we do–you do,” checks for understanding, feedback); use the lesson skeleton; keep brief notes on exit tickets and group needs.   
  • School Leaders — Select students; schedule rooms/periods; ensure BRIO administration; coordinate teacher prep; monitor group sizes/timetables. 
  • Support Teams (district/NGO/ministry) — Provide the package and templates; run short AFP orientations; help with diagnostics; collect anonymous implementation data for improvement.

Success metrics

Track a few simple indicators:

  • Coverage & participation: % of targeted students who received diagnostics, joined a group, and attended ≥75% of sessions.
  • Teaching practice: Teachers complete the AFP module and demonstrate weekly use of checks for understanding, feedback, and guided practice (spot checks/peer observations). 
  • Learning progress: Gains from BRIO baseline → post-diagnostic; improvement on mid-course unit checks (e.g., +25–30 percentage points on targeted skills).
  • Student confidence/engagement: Short pulse survey (3–4 items) on “math confidence” and “willingness to try harder problems.”
