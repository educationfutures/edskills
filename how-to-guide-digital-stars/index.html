How-to guide: Digital STARs

How to Implement the Digital STARs Program in Your Country

Who this guide is for

This guide is designed for librarians, school leaders, local government teams, NGOs, and national ministries who want to implement large-scale digital inclusion programs. It provides step-by-step instructions for delivering the Digital STARs Program in libraries and community hubs.

Before you start

  • Train the trainers: Identify master trainers who will prepare librarians and facilitators (20 national trainers → 700 librarians).
  • Set up learning hubs: Ensure libraries or local centers are equipped with internet, computers, and spaces for group learning.
  • Access the learning platform: Familiarize trainers and librarians with the STARs LMS, which includes course modules, self-reflection tools, and blended learning support.
  • Engage communities: Plan outreach campaigns targeting underrepresented groups (women, rural residents, Roma, pensioners, unemployed youth).

Step-by-step plan

Step 1 — Build the training cascade

What to do:

  • Train 20 national-level master trainers.
  • Master trainers prepare ~700 librarians/facilitators across Romania’s 42 counties.
  • Librarians deliver training directly to citizens.

Why it matters: A cascade model ensures scalability and sustainability, creating a pool of community mentors who continue to serve beyond the program.

Step 2 — Recruit and enroll citizens

What to do:

  • Run outreach campaigns through libraries, local authorities, schools, and NGOs.
  • Prioritize citizens with the lowest digital skills: rural residents, unemployed adults, older adults, and marginalized groups.
  • Register learners in the LMS, ensuring each has a profile and access credentials.

Why it matters: Inclusion depends on reaching those most at risk of digital exclusion, not only those already motivated to learn.

Step 3 — Deliver the 60-hour blended course

What to do:

  • Use the DigCompRo framework as the backbone (6 competence areas, 23 skills).
  • Deliver ~40 hours in person (hands-on practice, mentoring) and ~20 hours online (self-reflection, exercises).
  • Structure modules around three life domains: Personal Life (e.g., using online health services), Civic Life (e.g., accessing e-government), Professional Life (e.g., digital tools for job search and work).

Why it matters: Learners see immediate relevance, which improves motivation and retention.

Step 4 — Support librarians as facilitators

What to do:

  • Provide handbooks, coaching, and peer-learning opportunities for librarians.
  • Equip libraries with high-speed internet, modern computers, and assistive devices where possible.
  • Offer continuous mentoring through webinars and help desks.

Why it matters: Librarians are not just trainers; they act as digital mentors and trusted guides in their communities.

Step 5 — Monitor progress and evaluate impact

What to do:

  • Use the LMS self-reflection tool at the start, mid-point, and end of the course.
  • Track attendance, completion, and skills acquired.
  • Collect learner stories to capture qualitative impact.

Why it matters: Evaluation data show both personal empowerment (e.g., paying bills online) and systemic progress (e.g., increased use of e-government services).

Timeline examples

  • Pilot phase: 3–6 months with 2–3 counties.
  • Full rollout: 18 months to reach 100,000 learners across Romania, with national reporting milestones every quarter.

Roles and responsibilities

  • Master Trainers: Lead librarian training, coach facilitators, and ensure program fidelity.
  • Librarians/Facilitators: Deliver blended training, support learners, and collect local feedback.
  • National Ministry & World Bank partners: Provide oversight, platform support, and evaluation.
  • Local Authorities & NGOs: Assist with outreach, recruitment, and targeted inclusion.

Success metrics

  • Coverage: Number of librarians trained; number of learners enrolled per county.
  • Participation: Attendance and completion rates for the 60-hour course.
  • Skills gained: % of learners showing improvement in DigCompRo self-reflection surveys.
  • Inclusion: Share of participants from priority groups (women, rural, Roma, older adults).
  • Impact stories: Case studies of learners applying new skills in everyday life.
